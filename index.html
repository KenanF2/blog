Describing The Purpose of IT in Modern Business

Communication with Customers

Technology primarily affects the ability of a company to communicate with customers. In today's busy business environment, employees need to interact quickly and clearly with customers. Websites enable customers to find answers after hours to their queries. Fast shipping options enable businesses to move items over a broad geographic area. Business benefits when consumers use technology to communicate with a company because better communication develops a better public reputation.[2]

Efficiency of Operations

Technology also motivates a company to recognize it's requirements for revenue and maintain valuable resources like time and physical space. Warehouse inventory technologies enable business owners to comprehend how best to control a product's storage costs.With the correct systems in place, managers can also save money and time because instead of being at corporate headquarters they can save time by holding meetings over the Internet.[2]

Business Culture and Class Relations

Technology generates a vibrant squad within a company because staff at distinct places have stronger relationships.If factory executives can interact with delivery coordinators at a varying places, conflicts and suspicion are less probable to develop.Cliques and personal conflicts can become a nightmare for a company ; innovation often enables employees set aside their unique backgrounds.[2]

Security

Most modern-day companies are susceptible to threats to security and vandalism. Technology can be used to safeguard economic data, managerial private choices and other proprietary information leading to competitive benefits. Simply put, technology enables companies to keep their thoughts and ideas away from their competition.By getting computers with passwords, a company can guarantee that the competition will not copy any of its upcoming initiatives.[2]

Research Capacity

A company with the technological ability to search for new opportunities will remain a step ahead of its competition It needs to develop and gain fresh possibilities for a company to survive. The Internet enables a company to move into new economies without the expense of an executive plane or the dangers of setting up a factory overseas.[2]

Why do Digital Projects Fail?

Lack of expertise

Digital projects can fail due to a lack of expertise in the cross-functional aspects. Some make the mistake of believing that only “digital people” are needed for digital projects. However all digital projects have a need for someone with skills in business process management to assist the team in assessing and planning the alignment between the back-end operational processes and the customer journey.[3]

Limited digital strategy

A lot of digital projects are concentrated completely on the objective of a single initiative or department, instead of contributing to the company’s digital strategy and business goals overall. Specifically define what the project's success looks like and how it contributes to the business strategy before the company starts moving forward.[3]

Fixed Plans

The notion of a “5-year-plan” is a fixed plan that doesn’t account for change, which is not practical these days when other projects make improvements over time. Using iterative processes that are Agile and allow for frequent tweaks and improvement is the primary method to make sure that your success is lasts. Your digital project plans should be flexible enough to reflect business changes within the company and the market at any given moment.[3]

Overambition

Expecting the project to be perfect on the first build attempt instead of thinking that done is better than perfect can lead to projects that drag on way past their expected completion time. Instead you should try releasing a “Minimum Viable Product”, so consumers can have early versions of the project that gauge how the market will respond and determine if the concept is worth pursuing. Having a goal of releasing a perfect site in 3 months is not realistic without a real business plan trying to succeed this way is sure to fail.[3]

Steps for choosing software and IT systems that are right for your business

  1. Know your objective (or “What’s the point?”) Firstly you need to know what you want the software to be doing for you. How will the software save costs and save time on the project.
  2. Create a list of software requirements
    Functional requirements - List of what you need the software to do. [4]
    Usability requirements - Check if the software is easy enough to use for your team’s skill set/level and if these types of software are something they are used to or can get used to easily.[4]
    Technological preferences - Decide what kind of systems you want to work on like Mac or windows do you want it be stored on premises or in the cloud etc. You should have an Idea of where you want it.[4]
    Budgetary requirements. - Set a budget to know how much you’re willing to pay for the systems and know how much to pay for these systems to save your business money A.K.A Return on investment (ROI).[4]
    Reporting requirements - Decide whether you need reports from your systems and If so, what needs to be included? Should these be presented as dashboards, or emailed to you?[4]
    Scalability requirements. - What scale is your business at and do you expect this volume to increase over the next 2 to 5 years?[4]
    Vendor requirements - Make sure you know all the requirements of the supplier themselves. Perhaps this includes the support response times, current user volumes, years in business, or the vendor’s roadmap for future development.[4]
  3. Start searching for relevant applications - Start looking for apps and sites that may have information about you’re software because the most popular ones may not be the best fit for your project. Use reliable software comparison sites to get a list of competitors.[4]
  4. Start excluding solutions - You can start getting rid of software options that don’t meet your requirements, for example if the software only works on Windows and the team is working on Macs you can exclude that solution, also if the software is too expensive you can exclude it. Once the list has been whittled down you can trial the rest of the systems to see which one is the most suitable for your needs.[4]
  5. Start trialling your selected software - Trialling the software to check it against your usability requirements is the best way to see if a system is suitable for you. Find out the systems learnability, efficiency, satisfaction, errors and the memorability[4]
  6. Do your due diligence - You should know which systems you want to use so now you should investigate your resellers and address your vendor requirements that you have listed.[4]
  7. Start negotiating - At this stage this is where you start to finalise the finer details of the system. Make sure you address the business issues, such as, support levels, implementation times, iservice availability and payment terms – not just price. When talking final prices you should include likely costs as some resellers and vendors are more flexible on prices.[4]

GDPR’s impact on digital projects

Obtaining Consent

Active opt-in – Consent will require a positive opt-in through clear affirmative action. Pre-ticked boxes, for example, will no longer be compliant.[5] Granular – Blanket consent will not be compliant. Individuals should be able to know as much information about their data that is being processed as possible.The best way to ensure this would be to allow individuals to withdraw consent from each of the processing activities.[5] Unbundling – Consent should not be a precondition of signing up to a service unless necessary such as a newspaper subscription. Consent requests have to be separated from the terms and conditions.It should also be clear to the individual whether there will be third parties processing their data other than the people they are signing up to.[5]

Digital Marketing

The final area GDPR is set to impact digital marketing is data processing. Digital marketers will only be able to process the data they really require, they would need legal justification for the reason why they are processing the collected data.[5]

Constraints affecting websites

Website Colors - The colours used on your website are important, as your viewers may be able to see everything clearly on an LCD monitor may not be as clear and appealing when viewed on an older monitor. Choosing the wrong colour can also mean that some things are not readable, like having a bright coloured font that blends into the background would be bad for a website.[6] Typeface Limitations - Not all computers are able to display all fonts, so if a viewer comes to your page, that is displaying a font that is unavailable to them, they will be shown a substitute font. This substitute font can distort your page and throw off the design making it look not aesthetically pleasing.[6] Internet Limitations - Having really well designed web pages, with great animations and interactive options can be great, however this can make the page take a long time to download. People with slower connections to the internet will have a hard time running a website like this with so many features to download they may experience freezing and prolonged loading times, which will leave them dissatisfied.[6] Display Limitations - The website display itself can be seen as a constraint for the website. The design must comply with as many browsers as possible, not to mention making it mobile-friendly for viewers that are visiting from their phones. You can reach a bigger audience if the websites display works well for more people.[6] Copyright - A constraint to your website may be copyright, as there are media and information that are protected by copyrights. If you infringe these copyright laws you can receive fines of up to £50,000/$65,000 or even possibly face incarceration. However if you copyright your website people may still be able to use your media and information under fair-use and would require no permissions from you the owner of the copyrighted work.[6]

Accessibility Issues

Visually impaired - To cater to the visually impaired you can make sure colours are well contrasted and easily differentiable to people with colorblindness. It is also a good idea to use more than one differentiator, for example, colour and underline or block capitals. Dynamic type lettering (lettering that can be increased in size to suit the use) is the simplest way you can make your website accessible to low-vision users. Hearing impaired - Having captions or subtitles for media like videos and audio files would be an important part of catering to the hearing impaired and make it easier for them to access things on your web page. It is important to keep descriptions brief and use lots of verbs. There are web extensions you can use to get a report on your site. They can run a multitude of tests and report back any problems but they may not catch everything so it is important you try user testing and see if there are any problems you can notice for yourself. Motor disability - Including menus and any other choices available on a site can be difficult for people with motor disabilities if the buttons and links are too small so making larger touch buttons is a huge help to people with motor disease. Single touch options on your website would also be very beneficial.
